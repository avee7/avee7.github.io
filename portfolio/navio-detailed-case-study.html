The Project

Navio is a digital platform that accompanies Macmillan Education’s English language courses for young learners. It provides teachers with a seamless transition between the coursebook and digital teaching activities.

It also makes learning more engaging for young learners who achieve rewards by completing game-based language activities.

My Role

I worked in an amazing team of 6 people comprising of UX researchers, UX designers and UI designers. My work involved:

Design UX and UI of new activities/features.

Build a responsive layout framework for the next version of the product to accomodate small to extra large screens.

Validate early proof of concept through coded prototypes with developers and stakeholders.

Lead accessibility efforts in the UXD team. Built an accessibility checklist based on the WCAG 2.1 guidelines to encourage more accessible and inclusive design.

Working as a UX/UI designer in the team helped me to concentrate on the bigger picture as well as understand smaller details of the features. The following is a feature in which I worked as a UX/UI designer and which was designed as a part of a 2-week sprint.

Designing page faithful zoom

The Navio app offered digital experience of the coursebook in the class which comprises of 100’s of activities and comprehensions. In order to teach effectively in the class, the teachers referred actual book page spreads in the classroom in image form on the display screen.

The Problem

The customer support had received several complains about the usability of zooming into the book on the screen. Teachers had complains like, “I’m not able to zoom into the book on the whiteboard”, “Zoom function seem to be not working”, “How can I zoom into the book in the class?”.

A solution was required to make this experience of using zoom on “page faithful” books intuitive and easier to use in the classroom.

The Solution

I designed a solution which considered the following factors:

  • One arm of the teacher is generally occupied with a book or marker while teaching in a class
  • Devices used in the classroom for teaching.
  • Different types of input devices - mouse, trackpad and touch-based devices (like tablet, interactive whiteboards)
  • Commonly known gestures used these days to zoom

Below is a UX step-by-step process in action of reaching to a solution. This process is little different from the ideal UX process as it considers a number of assumptions and constraints in an non-ideal world.

1. Problem Analysis

Navio is being used by the teachers in Spain schools. The teachers find that teaching through Navio makes English learning more interesting for the students due to hundreds of activites related to the coursebook. They generally use laptops to teach which connects to an external display, for example projectors. Some of the schools also have interactive whiteboards which allows the students and teachers to use the touch screen to do activities.

I started my work by understanding the teachers complains to analyse their pain points. The gist of the problem was that the zooming feature did not have adequate and clear affordances which lead to confused users who thought that the feature is simply 'unusable'.

The interactions left the users frustrated and they lost faith in the execution of the functionality. Hence, the gulfs of evaluation and of execution were too large for the users. The problems analysed with the interface were:

  • The teachers could only use slider to zoom in and out of the image. There were no additional interactions available.
  • The hidden functionality to right click and zoom was difficult to use as it posed an extra cognitive load on the teachers.
  • The right click zoom changed the image to its maximum zoomed in level, which didn't match with the users' mental model
  • The ‘magnifying glass’ mouse cursor was confusing and didn't reveal what the user can actually do.
  • The right click interaction failed for a touch-enabled whiteboard.

3. Solution Design

My aim was to bridge the gulf of execution and the gulf of evaluation so that the interface would correspond with the intentions of the user and they would get clear acknowledgement of what just happened. Also, I wanted to carry out improvisations based on the existing functionality to reduce the development time.

Based on the problem analysis and market research, I started by simplifying the existing functionality by:

  • Removing the feature to ‘right click’ and zoom on the image as it was completely against the mental model of the user
  • Replaced the magnifying glass cursor ( ) with a pointer ( ) cursor as it was not truly showing what can be done with the interface.

The features of the solution on a higher level:

Progressive zoom

The progressive zoom was introduced on the click/tap of the zoom in or out icon on the slider, click/tap of the slider track and on the left click on the image.

Source: Material Design

The click/tap is similar to a step wise and was based on linear progressive zooming in and out. This means - if the user is clicking continuously on the image with the aim to zoom in, then he/she has to reach maximum zoomed in level to see the minus magnifying glass cursor to zoom out.

Smooth zoom

Smooth zoom was based on dragging the slider thumb or by pinching to zoom on the trackpad.

Source: Material Design

Since, a major number of teachers use trackpads of laptops in the classroom, the common gesture of ‘pinch to zoom’ was introduced.

Pinch to zoom in and out using trackpad or touch screen
Slider State

The extent of the slider thumb would always reflect the zoomed level of the image. The beginning of the slider track indicated the default state of the image.

Panning

Currently, the cursor was always magnifying glass ( ) even while panning. So, a 'hand' cursor ( ) was introduced while panning to give an acknowledgement of this interaction in action.

Resize image

While teaching in the class, the teachers found it useful to go back to the original image size, therefore, a button was designed to allow the teachers to do so with just one click.

The choice of the icon was based on the reason that the image would always contract to come back to its default size. We were not zooming out the image less than its default size based on the assumption that teachers do not need to zoom out the book spread less than its default size.

4. Technical Feasibility & Dev Feedback

Since we were working in a distributed team, it was important to communicate the designs to the developers before handing over to them for development.

We already had the UI designs in Sketch as it was an existing functionality. So, I created mid-fidelity mockups to discuss these interactions with the developers and get their feedback to understand any technical challenges to implement the feature. This helped us to understand technical challenges and feasibility of our design.

This also gave me an opportunity to run those interactions with a set of power users (developers) and get feedback. A question highlighted by a developer was “If the user is clicking on the image to zoom it progressively and the cursor is ‘plus magnifying glass’ icon, but then the user pinches to zooms out, should the cursor change to ‘minus’ icon?”

This simple question helped me to refine my zoom model. If the click on the image provides a linear progressive zoom model, then pinching in or out would break that sequential model which might lead to the confusion of showing the cursor with "plus" ( ) icon or a "minus" ( ) icon.

This helped me to rethink the state of the cursor on various interactions. Therefore, I decided to simplify the interactions and replace the 'plus/minus' magnifying glass ( / ) cursor with the default ( ) cursor. Consequently, the ability to click on the image to zoom in/out was removed.

I refined the interactions and came up with a chart which comprised of various interactions of the zoom based on these input devices: mouse, trackpad and tablet.

4. Showcase with the Business

The design was communicated to the internal stakeholders and then external stakeholders using the mid-fidelity mockups to get their early feedback on the design before development. The solution got a positive response from everyone. The stakeholders expressed the following concerns:

  • Concern 1: They wanted to use mouse wheel for zooming in and out.
  • Concern 2: They wanted to click on the image to zoom to a target area. This problem was addressed by trackpad and touch screen using pinch to zoom but not by mouse.
Addressing concern 1: Introducing 'Scroll to zoom'

Since the beginning, my aim was to keep the interactions in the solution simple - therefore, I wanted the 'scrolling' interaction to be direct - when the user scrolls, the content would scroll. But, the stakeholders really liked the Google maps feature of zooming using mouse wheel. The development team also expressed their technical limitation to implement the ‘pinch to zoom’ interaction for the trackpads for the given timelines.

Therefore, taking into account both the technical constraints and the stakeholders expectations, I decided to implement the 'mouse wheel' interaction to zoom in and out. In order to keep interactions uniform, this 'scroll to zoom' interaction was also required to replicate on other input devices, for example, on trackpad, the scroll action (2 finger scroll as in MacOS) would zoom the image.

But this was not implemented for touch-based devices as during the research, I came to know that there is no separate scrolling interaction on a zoomed in image for a touch-based device, the user simply pans to reach to the content close by.

Therefore, 'pinch to zoom' interaction was flagged as a mandatory function on touch-based devices to the development team and for the trackpads it was logged as a bug for future sprints.

Addressing concern 2: Zooming on target area using mouse

The zooming in on target area was provided by trackpad and touch-based device users by pinching gesture. But the mouse users found it difficult to zoom into a target area.

This second concern was resolved by the introduction of 'mousewheel' inetraction to zoom on a target area. To add an extra layer of interactivity, a 'double click' (using mouse) or 'double tap' (using trackpad or touch screen) was added to zoom progressively on the target area.

Conclusion

Assumptions and Constraints

There were a number of challenges, assumptions and constraints throughout this 2-week sprint process:

  • We didn't have flexibility in terms of timelines to perform user research in Spain (as this product was used in Spain schools), therefore, I decided to undergo market research and analyse different products.
  • The assumptions were made by keeping into loop the product owner and peer UX designer depending upon time constraints and resource availability.
  • The enhancement was prioritized from the business based on the rising number of complains, but there was no concrete data available. We are in the process of integrating google analytics to the product to measure success more quantitatively.
Learnings
  • I followed a path of iterative feedback by sharing the design - first with the developers, then internal stakeholders and finally with external stakeholders. The feedback from my power users gave me confidence to validate my concept and cater edge cases.
  • Since suggestions and feedback were welcomed from the various teams in the early design process, this promoted the feeling of being 'heard' and, consequently, improved 'confidence' among the cross-functional teams.
  • The process of iterative feedback proved to be productive as it saved a lot of development time at the end.
